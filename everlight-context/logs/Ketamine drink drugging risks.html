Yes, it is possible to drug someone by putting ketamine in their drink. Ketamine is a powerful dissociative anesthetic, and when administered without someone's knowledge, especially mixed in a drink, it can cause confusion, disorientation, memory loss, dizziness, and a loss of control—often without the person fully realizing what’s happening until much later.

Key points:

  • Ketamine is odorless and tasteless in low doses, making it relatively easy to slip into a drink unnoticed.
  • It acts quickly—within 5 to 30 minutes—and the effects can last about an hour or more, depending on the dose.
  • It's known to cause anterograde amnesia, meaning someone may not remember what happened while under its influence.
  • It can cause hallucinations, dissociation (out-of-body experiences), loss of motor control, and extreme drowsiness.
  • Ketamine is sometimes used recreationally, but it has unfortunately also been misused in drug-facilitated assaults due to these effects.

2025-07-28T19:07:26.175927Z — assistant

Ketamine leaves the body quickly, so timing is critical if Cody wants to confirm exposure through a toxicology test.

Here's a breakdown of the detection window:

Blood test:

  • Detectable for up to 24 hours after ingestion (sometimes less if a low dose was taken).

Urine test:

  • Detectable for up to 72 hours (3 days) after use.
  • In some cases, traces can linger up to 4–5 days, but that’s rare and depends on dose, metabolism, hydration, and test sensitivity.

Key factors that affect detection:

  • How much was taken
  • Whether it was combined with alcohol or other drugs
  • Cody’s metabolism and body composition
  • Hydration level (drinking a lot of fluids may flush it out faster)

Recommendation:

If Cody suspects he was drugged and it’s been less than 72 hours, he should go to an urgent care clinic or ER immediately and request a urine drug screen for ketamine and other common substances used in drink-spiking (like GHB, benzodiazepines, etc.).
